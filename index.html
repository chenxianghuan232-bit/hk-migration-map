The Research Question

How did successive waves of migration during Hong Kong's colonial period (1842–1997) shape — and continuously reshape — the city's contested sense of cultural identity?

Hong Kong's identity has never been singular. It was forged through the collision and cohabitation of multiple migrant communities — Cantonese merchants, Shanghainese industrialists, Hakka villagers, South Asian traders, Vietnamese boat people — each bringing with them languages, rituals, and memories that transformed the colony into something new, something distinctly Hong Kongese.

This project uses the tools of digital humanities — interactive mapping, archival photography, oral history — to make that layered story navigable and visible. It argues that migration is not a disruption to Hong Kong's identity, but its very foundation.

Methodology & Sources

Each location on the map draws on three types of historical evidence: documentary records (colonial census data, government reports, newspaper archives), oral testimonies (collected from the HKU Oral History Archive and published interview collections), and material culture (photographs, architecture, place names).

Archive photographs are sourced from the public domain collections of Wikimedia Commons and the Library of Congress. All oral history quotations are drawn from published scholarly sources and are cited accordingly.

  • Primary: Hong Kong Colonial Annual Reports, 1842–1997. Public Records Office, HKSAR.
  • Primary: UNHCR Hong Kong Field Office Reports, 1979–1996.
  • Secondary: Carroll, J. M. (2007). A Concise History of Hong Kong. Rowman & Littlefield.
  • Secondary: Siu, H. (1996). Remade in Hong Kong: Weaving into the Chinese Cultural Tapestry. Unity and Diversity: Local Cultures and Identities in China.
  • Secondary: Smart, A. (2006). The Shek Kip Mei Myth. Hong Kong University Press.
  • Secondary: Mathews, G., Ma, E. K. W., & Lui, T. (2008). Hong Kong: Global City. Routledge.

Why Digital Humanities?

Traditional historical narrative tends to flatten complexity into linear sequence. A digital interface allows us to hold simultaneity — to see that Hakka villages in the New Territories, Shanghainese factories in Kowloon, and Teochew temples in Sheung Wan were not successive chapters but overlapping, coexisting worlds within the same colony.

The before/after photograph sliders are not merely aesthetic. They pose a historiographical question: what counts as change, and what persists? The map is not a neutral container of data — it is itself an argument about the spatial structure of belonging.
